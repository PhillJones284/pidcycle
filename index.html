The PID-optimised research cycle

What's this?

This diagram represents an ideal PID-optimised research cycle. Here, persistent identifiers are registered, used, and shared at every stage. PIDs can be created for all people, places, projects, and things associated with research. Along with metadata, PIDs are collected and used by funders, institutions, and publishers, all at the earliest possible point in the process.

Crossref was created in 1999 by a group of 12 academic publishers and incorporated as the Publishers International Linking Association (PILA). It was the first collaborative reference linking system and official Digital Object Identifier (DOI) Registration Agency. Crossref DOIs cover a variety of content types, including journals, books, conference proceedings, working papers, technical reports, and data sets.

Crossref is a not-for-profit association with over 15,000 organisational members from 140 countries. The crossref database contains over 135 million items with associated metadata (as of July 2021).

DataCite is a non-profit organisation that provides persistent identifiers (DOIs) for research data and other research outputs. It is an official Digital Object Identifier (DOI) Registration Agency of the International DOI Foundation. Organizations within the research community join DataCite as members to be able to assign DOIs. This way, their outputs become discoverable and associated metadata is made available to the community.

DataCite members include data centers, libraries, government agencies, research universities and more from 42 different countries.

Click here to learn more about DataCite.

  Research Activity Identifier

RAID is the youngest of the five priority PIDs. Created and funded by Australian Research Data Commons (ARDC), it is a unique and persistent identifier for research projects. It acts as a container for research project activities by collecting identifiers for the people, publications, instruments and institutions that are involved.

RAiD makes use of the handle system (the same technology that underpins DOI) and records time and date stamps for funders, organisations, collaborators, and research outputs.

Click here to learn more about RAiD.

ORCID stands for Open Researcher and Contributor ID and is the name of both a nonproprietary alphanumeric code to uniquely identify authors and contributors of scholarly communication and the nonprofit organization that oversees the creation and maintenance of the IDs. ORCID provides a variety of metadata powered services including ORCID profiles, which are a type of online persistent CV, and a member APId which facilitates the registration and metadata exchange between organisations’ information systems and ORCID records.

ORCID began as a collaborative effort by publishers of scholarly research "to resolve the author name ambiguity problem in scholarly communication”. It currently has over 1,100 members, including research organizations, publishers, funders, professional associations, and other stakeholders.

Click here to learn more about ORCID.

X

The Research Organisation Registry (ROR) is a community-led project to develop an open, sustainable, usable, and unique identifier for every research organization in the world. It was founded in 2019 following a collaborative effort between 17 organisations specifically to address the problem of a lack of community-governed nonproprietary persistent identifier for research performing organisations.

In the future, ROR plans to become self-sustaining through the provision of paid services on top of its core, free identifier offering. In the meantime, it is sustained through in-kind support from its governing organisations, donations and grants.

Click here to learn more about ROR.

1. Grant Application

Researchers and institutions pass PIDs for previous grants, outputs, organisations, people, and projects to grant application systems

BENEFITS:
  1. Less manual data entry
  2. More accurate data
  3. More time for including contextual information
2. Grant application and review

Funders ingest data about grants, outputs, organisations, and projects from PID registries

BENEFITS:
  1. Less manual data entry
  2. More accurate data
  3. More time for including contextual information
  4. Easier reviewer selection and recognition
3. Grant award

Funders register DOIs for new grant metadata

BENEFITS:
  1. Globally unique grant IDs and metadata to aid reporting
  2. Authoritative metadata and connections to other PIDs to aid project management
  3. Simplified communication of and compliance with policies (e.g. OA, DMPs, etc.)
4. Grant award

Institutions ingest data about grants and associated people and organisations

BENEFITS:
  1. More accurate data
  2. Time/effort savings
  3. Improved reporting
5. Project registration

Institutions register RAiDs for projects and add/update links to associated grants, equipment, people, and organisations

BENEFITS:
  1. More accurate data
  2. Time/effort savings
  3. Improved visibility of collaborations across institutions and countries
6. Output submission

Researchers share their ORCID ID when submitting new outputs and connect to ROR IDs for institutional affiliations and grant DOIs for funding

BENEFITS:
  1. More accurate data about co-authors, affiliations, and funding
  2. Time/effort savings in review and metadata creation
  3. Easier sign-in
  4. Easier APC management
  5. Easier reviewer selection and recognition
7. Output publication

Publisher/repositories ingest data about grants, people, projects, and organisations linked to outputs

BENEFITS:
  1. Reduced administrative overhead for publishers
  2. Improved author experience
  3. Richer metadata to aid discovery, analysis, and reporting
8. Output registration

Publishers/repositories register DOIs for new outputs and populate metadata

BENEFITS:
  1. Globally unique output IDs and metadata to aid analysis, citation, discovery, and reporting
  2. Embedded PIDs for associated entities (authors, funding, etc.) streamline reporting and analysis
  3. Greater ability to track reach and impact of content
9. Content notifications

PID registries send automatic updates of new publications etc. to institutions and funders

BENEFITS:
  1. Reduced administrative overhead for institutions/funders
  2. More timely data for reporting and analysis
  3. Less manual data entry
  4. More accurate data
  5. Time/effort savings for researchers
10. Reporting

Researchers and institutions pass PIDs for outputs, organisations, people, and projects to funders' reporting systems

BENEFITS:
  1. Less manual data entry
  2. More accurate data
  3. More time for including contextual information
